**Title**: Lens: A Locally Hosted, LoRA Fine-Tuned Retrieval-Augmented Generation System for CS/AI Research Paper Discovery and Synthesis 

## Abstract /Summary of proposal: 
Lens is a locally hosted intelligent research assistant for computer science and artificial intelligence literature. The system fetches papers from arXiv, processes and indexes them using semantic embeddings and FAISS vector search, and answers natural language research questions with synthesised, citation-backed responses. The core research contribution is the fine-tuning of a small open-source language model, specifically Microsoft Phi-3 or Meta LLaMA 3, using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) on a curated dataset of CS/AI paper abstracts and question-answer pairs. The project investigates whether a locally fine-tuned small language model can match or approach the synthesis quality of a large cloud-hosted model such as GPT-4o on academic paper question answering, while running entirely offline at zero recurring cost. This has direct practical implications for students and researchers in resource-constrained environments where reliable internet and API budgets cannot be guaranteed.

## Problem Statements: 
1. **Navigating the volume of CS/AI research published on arXiv natively using keyword search is ineffective.** 
   Computer science publications are projected to reach 580,000 annually by 2025, driven primarily by advances in artificial intelligence, and this exponential growth makes it increasingly difficult for researchers and scholars to find relevant work amid the resulting information overload (arXiv survey, 2025). The number of papers submitted to the core AI categories of arXiv doubles roughly every two years, a trajectory that has already outpaced any individual researcher's capacity to keep pace through manual reading (ResearchGate, 2022). Traditional keyword search retrieves documents containing matching terms but cannot reason about meaning, relevance, or conceptual relationships between papers. Lens addresses this through semantic vector search over FAISS-indexed paper embeddings, enabling retrieval based on meaning rather than surface form. 

2. **General-purpose LLMs hallucinate with domain-specific research questions.** 
   Large language models exhibit hallucination, outdated knowledge, and non-transparent reasoning, and while retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) reduces these failures by grounding responses in retrieved documents, limitations in both the retrieval and generation components can still produce confabulated outputs (Gao et al., 2024). A systematic review of hallucination in RAG systems confirmed that insufficient retrieval quality and model deviation from retrieved evidence remain the two primary sources of factual error in RAG pipelines, even when the correct source documents are present in the index (MDPI Mathematics, 2025). In a research context, a single fabricated citation or misattributed finding can invalidate an entire literature review. Lens mitigates this by combining precise semantic retrieval with a domain fine-tuned local model trained specifically on CS/AI academic text, reducing the distributional gap between training data and retrieval context. 

3. **Cloud-hosted LLM APIs introduce cost, data privacy risk, and vendor dependency.** 
   Concerns about data privacy, vendor lock-in, and long-term operating costs are among the primary drivers of interest in local LLM deployment, and recent evidence shows that for specialised domain tasks a compact model fine-tuned on relevant data can achieve comparable accuracy to a general-purpose LLM at a fraction of the computational cost (arXiv cost-benefit analysis, 2025). LoRA resolves the hardware barrier directly: by freezing pre-trained model weights and injecting small trainable rank-decomposition matrices into each transformer layer, it reduces trainable parameters by up to 10,000 times and GPU memory requirements by a factor of three, achieving performance on par with or better than full fine-tuning across multiple benchmarks (Hu et al., 2022). Together, these two developments mean that fine-tuning a model such as Phi-3 mini on a domain-specific academic dataset is achievable on a single consumer GPU or via free cloud compute. Lens is the first student-built system to combine LoRA fine-tuning with a full RAG pipeline specifically for CS/AI research paper synthesis, and its comparative evaluation between the fine-tuned local model and a cloud baseline constitutes the project's original research contribution. 

## Aim: 
1. To design, build, and evaluate a locally hosted research assistant that ingests CS/AI papers from arXiv, indexes them using semantic embeddings and FAISS, and uses a LoRA fine-tuned small language model running entirely offline to answer research questions with cited, synthesis-quality responses, evaluating whether a fine-tuned local model can approach cloud-model quality at zero recurring cost.

## Objectives: SMART – Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time Bound 
1. To build a data ingestion pipeline that fetches CS/AI papers from the arXiv API, extracts and cleans text using PyMuPDF, chunks documents using LangChain's RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter, generates embeddings using the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 sentence transformer, and stores a searchable FAISS index covering a minimum of 200 papers. 
2. To implement a semantic search and RAG question-answering module that retrieves the top-k most relevant paper chunks via cosine similarity and passes them as context to a locally running base model via Ollama, achieving a minimum Precision@5 of 0.70 on a manually curated set of 30 test queries. 
3. To construct a fine-tuning dataset of at least 500 (abstract, research question, ideal answer) triplets drawn from CS/AI arXiv papers and fine-tune Phi-3 mini or LLaMA 3 8B using LoRA via the Hugging Face PEFT library on Google Colab, producing a documented LoRA adapter checkpoint with training loss curves. 
4. To integrate the LoRA fine-tuned model into the Lens RAG pipeline and conduct a comparative evaluation against the base model and GPT-4o-mini using ROUGE-L scores and a 20-question human evaluation rubric assessing factual accuracy, citation relevance, and response coherence. 
5. To deliver a complete, locally runnable CLI system with full documentation and write a final project report covering the architecture, fine-tuning methodology, evaluation results, and a structured discussion of the trade-offs between locally fine-tuned small models and cloud-hosted APIs for academic research assistance.
